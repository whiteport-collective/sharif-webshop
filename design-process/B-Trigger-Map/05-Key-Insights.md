## Primary Development Focus

1. **Harriet's Flow is Sacred** — Every design decision starts with "does this make Harriet's experience harder?" If yes, don't do it.
2. **Speed Over Features** — Under 3 minutes from landing to payment. Cut anything that adds steps.
3. **The Experience IS the Product** — One continuous parallax surface, not a store with pages. This is the unfair advantage no competitor can quickly copy.
4. **AI Operations From Day One** — Claude Cowork replaces CMS. The demo must show Moohsen this works.
5. **EV Content is the SEO Goldmine** — Norway's 80%+ EV new car sales create a content gap nobody is filling.

---

## Critical Success Factors

- **The Parallax Flow:** Must feel smooth, fast, and guided — not like a website. This is what makes the demo irresistible and the product defensible.
- **Visual Dimension Guide:** Harriet has never looked at a tire sidewall. The guide must make this feel obvious, not educational.
- **Price Transparency:** Show the price clearly at every step. No surprises. No hidden mounting fees.
- **EU Label Storytelling:** Transform raw data (D/C/70dB) into human language ("Solid everyday tire, good in rain"). The AI content agent's killer feature.
- **Post-Payment Booking:** Don't risk cart abandonment with scheduling. Payment first, booking second.

---

## Design Implications

### The Guided Flow Must:
- Show ONE thing per step — never overload
- Animate transitions with parallax depth — forward feels like moving deeper
- Default sort products by price (cheapest first — Harriet's world)
- Collapse filters by default (Ole expands if he wants)
- Never use tire jargon without immediate plain-language translation

### Product Display Must:
- Lead with price, image, and plain-language description
- Show EU labels as visual sliders, not letters
- Include AI-generated product story per SKU
- Show live stock status via widget (in stock / low / out of stock)
- Product pages persist when out of stock (SEO value)

### The Dimension Input Must:
- Be the first and only thing on screen
- Include a visual guide to finding the numbers
- Use three simple dropdowns (width / profile / rim)
- Feel inviting, not intimidating

### The Payment Hand-off Must:
- Be a clean cut — big "Betal na" button
- Lead to Klarna/Qliro (not a custom checkout)
- Handle private and business purchases natively
- Feel like a natural end to the flow, not an interruption

### The Booking Step Must:
- Feel rewarding ("Takk! Finn din tid!")
- Show available slots from Google Calendar
- Be simple — pick shop, pick time, confirm
- If she doesn't book immediately, email reminder follows

## Design Focus Statement

**The Sharif.no experience transforms Norwegian car owners from resigned local-shop customers into empowered online tire buyers who get the best price with zero effort, through a continuous guided dialog — not a store.**

**Primary Design Target:** Harriet the Hairdresser

**Must Address (Critical for Conversion):**
1. Fear of looking stupid → No jargon, visual guides, plain language
2. Fear of wrong choice → Dimension input ensures fit, only matching tires shown
3. Fear of wasting time → Under 3 minutes, no rabbit holes
4. Want: speed → Continuous flow, no page reloads
5. Want: fair price → Cheapest first, transparent pricing

**Should Address (Supporting Conversion):**
1. Ole needs EV-relevant specs → EU label sliders, load rating visibility
2. Ole needs brand options → Collapsed filter, available when needed
3. Trust signals → 60+ year brand, real shops, real addresses
4. Multi-channel → API for AI agent purchasing (ChatGPT, Claude)
5. Multi-language → Norwegian + English

## Cross-Group Patterns

### Shared Drivers
- **Fair pricing** — all three personas want to feel they're getting a good deal
- **No wasted time** — Harriet wants 2 min, Ole wants 10 min, Magnus wants to find his exact tire fast
- **Trust** — all need to believe the tires are correct, safe, and properly handled

### Unique Drivers
- **Harriet:** Zero knowledge accommodation — the flow must require no tire expertise
- **Ole:** EV-specific intelligence — the system must understand what his Tesla needs
- **Magnus:** Brand specificity — he knows exactly what he wants and won't settle

### Potential Tensions
- **Harriet vs. Ole:** Harriet wants minimal options, Ole wants filters and specs. **Resolution:** Filters collapsed by default. Harriet sees sorted list, Ole expands for detail.
- **Harriet vs. Magnus (V2):** Budget products vs. premium brands in the same catalog. **Resolution:** Default sort by price serves Harriet. Brand filter serves Magnus. They never interfere with each other.
